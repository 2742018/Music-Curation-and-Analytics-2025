Task 1 – Chroma-Based Similarity Between Tracks

For this task, I reused the Jupyter notebook from the group lab and adapted it to my own theme. I replaced the original country category with an orchestral category and changed tracks 7–9 so that they are Zelda soundtrack cues taken from my Week 9 dataset (e.g. Gerudo Valley, Midna’s Lament, and Kakariko Village (Night)). For each track, I extracted chroma features in SonicVisualizer, exported them as CSV, and used the notebook to compute summary statistics and a similarity matrix.

The .ipynb file, along with all the images and CSVs, can be found in the 'data' folder: My Github 'data' Repository.

Chroma Features for All Tracks

The panel of chroma heatmaps above shows how pitch-class energy is distributed over time for each track. Track 7 appears dense and noisy, with energy distributed broadly across pitch classes—evidence of its continuous harmonic texture and lack of discrete chord changes. Track 8 shows repeating horizontal bands, revealing recurring harmonic material that cycles throughout the track. Track 9 features more vertical streaks than the others, indicating more explicit chord articulations and a more rhythmically driven harmonic structure. In contrast, some of the other tracks show more diffuse patterns.

Average Chroma Profiles by Track

Averaging the chroma values over time yields a pitch-class profile for each track. Track 7 displays an unusually flat chroma profile, indicating that no single pitch class dominates the track. This aligns with its atmospheric Zelda-style soundtrack, which relies more on evolving textures than on clear harmonic centres. Track 8, by contrast, shows distinct peaks around C, E, and B, reflecting a more stable minor-key centre characteristic of emotional Zelda themes. Track 9 presents a scattered pattern of chroma values, suggesting more frequent harmonic movement and subtle modal shifts throughout the piece.

Pitch-Class Scatter by Category

This scatter plot compares how often the pitch classes C and A occur across the ten tracks. The “orchestral” points sit between the more varied “classical” group and the strongly tonal “rock” group. This shows that the Zelda tracks rely on moderate, balanced pitch-class usage typical of atmospheric orchestral music rather than the strong tonal centres seen in rock.

Chroma-Based Similarity Matrix

The similarity matrix visualises how close each track is to the others based on chroma features. Darker colours indicate higher similarity. Tracks 7–9 (the Zelda soundtracks) cluster closely together, showing that they share strong harmonic and timbral characteristics. They appear clearly separated from the other tracks, which confirms their distinctive musical identity within the dataset. This shows that the Zelda orchestral-style tracks tend to share instrumentation, harmonic language, and timbral qualities. In contrast, tracks outside the Zelda theme show greater stylistic and harmonic diversity, reflected in broader variation in the matrix.

Task 2: Transcription Comparison

This task revisits my Week 2 score for Great Fairy Fountain. I exported the MuseScore file as a WAV, opened it in SonicVisualizer, ran the automatic note transcription, converted the result to MIDI, and uploaded it back to MuseScore. The images below compare my original notation with the transcription generated purely from audio.

The SonicVisualizer transcription captures the broad melodic outline of Great Fairy Fountain Etude, but a closer comparison reveals significant losses in harmonic, rhythmic, and expressive detail. The original score features flowing left-hand arpeggios, layered inner voices, and expressive markings that shape the piece’s ethereal character. In contrast, the transcription tends to flatten these textures. Rapid arpeggiated figures often collapse into single-note pulses, and the harmonic support that defines the piece’s shimmering atmosphere is largely absent.

Rhythmically, SonicVisualizer struggles with tuplets and ornamental gestures. Triplet figures appear as irregular note clusters or are omitted entirely, and grace-note gestures disappear, removing the anticipatory character they contribute in the original. The fundamental rhythmic values also become imprecise when multiple layers occur simultaneously, indicating difficulty in separating polyphonic lines from audio data.

Pitch accuracy is also inconsistent. Sustained harmonies in the original are reduced to fragmented notes, and chord voicings are frequently incomplete or misinterpreted. High-register embellishments occasionally register as noise, producing spurious pitches. Dynamic and expressive markings—mp espressivo, accent patterns, and poco a poco cresc.—are not represented at all, leaving the transcription mechanically flat.

Overall, the automated transcription provides a rough melodic framework but fails to capture the pianistic detail and structural clarity of the engraved score. This highlights the limitations of audio-to-MIDI tools for complex, multi-layered piano textures.
