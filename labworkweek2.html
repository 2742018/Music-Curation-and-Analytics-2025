Week 2 – Music as Notation

Optical Music Recognition and score correction using Audiveris and MuseScore.

Digitisation and Editing of a Printed Score

Introduction

This week’s task focused on responsibility in music librarianship and archival practice: The digitisation of a printed score for long-term preservation and future analysis. Using my selected theme— Koji Kondo’s compositions from The Legend of Zelda—I identified an appropriate piece, processed it using Optical Music Recognition (OMR), and corrected the resulting notation in MuseScore.

1. Selection and Acquisition of the Score

For this activity, I selected the piano etude version of Great Fairy Fountain by Koji Kondo from The Legend of Zelda Series, arranged by Erik Correll 'Piano Man'.

This piece was sourced from a publicly accessible, engraved PDF transcription on MuseScore. Its clear and consistent formatting made it highly suitable for OMR processing. For this activity, I focused specifically on transcribing and correcting bars 1–17 of the score.

2. Digitisation Using Audiveris (OMR)

The printed PDF was imported into Audiveris OMR, which automatically analysed its musical symbols and attempted to generate a corresponding digital notation file.

2.1 Accuracy of OMR Output

The OMR engine accurately recognised several structural elements, including:

  • Staff and system layout
  • Clefs and initial time signatures
  • A significant portion of the pitch material
  • Barline positions and measure divisions
Screenshot Placeholder: Audiveris interface showing recognised notation

However, several transcription errors occurred, consistent with known limitations of OMR systems:

  • Dotted rhythms were frequently misinterpreted, often recognised as undotted values or incorrectly as tied notes.
  • Rests were inconsistently detected, leading to missing or extraneous rests in certain measures.
  • Slurs and ties were not reliably captured, resulting in disrupted phrase continuity.
  • Accidentals occasionally failed to register or were applied incorrectly within the measure.
  • Beat distribution was sometimes inaccurate, producing measures with incorrect total durations.

These issues required manual review and correction to preserve the accuracy of the musical information.

3. Editing and Correcting the Score in MuseScore

The OMR output was exported as a MusicXML file and subsequently opened in MuseScore for systematic correction. MuseScore provided a flexible environment for editing notation, enabling both visual inspection and auditory verification through playback.

Screenshot Placeholder: MuseScore view of the imported MusicXML file

3.1 Corrections Performed

I edited more than fifteen bars, focusing on restoring the correct rhythmic and pitch structures. Key corrections included:

  • Re-entering notes with incorrect pitch or duration.
  • Fixing measures with erroneous beat totals to align with the time signature.
  • Reinstating missing rests and removing incorrectly interpreted ones.
  • Adding slurs, ties, and dynamics where they had been omitted.
  • Clarifying phrase structure and articulation.

3.2 Observations on MuseScore Functionality

Strengths

  • The note-input tools were intuitive and efficient for re-entering material.
  • Playback functionality facilitated checking rhythmic and pitch accuracy.
  • The interface allowed precise adjustments to slurs, dynamics, and articulations.
  • Zoom and inspection tools supported detailed verification of the transcription.

Challenges

  • Measures corrupted by OMR errors occasionally required deleting and reconstructing entire bars.
  • Some MusicXML elements were interpreted inconsistently, requiring additional clean-up.
  • Re-aligning ties and slurs demanded multiple steps to maintain correct phrasing.
Screenshot Placeholder: corrected measures within MuseScore

4. Reflection

This digitisation exercise highlighted the interplay between automated recognition systems and human editorial oversight in music preservation. While Audiveris OMR provided an efficient starting point, the inaccuracies in its output underscored the limitations of current OMR technology, particularly when applied to complex musical textures or non-standard engraving practices.

The subsequent correction process in MuseScore demonstrated the essential role of manual verification and interpretive decision-making in archival work. Through this task, I developed a deeper understanding of how musical information is encoded, the fragility of automated transcription workflows, and the necessity of accurate digital representation for future scholarly analysis and long-term preservation.
