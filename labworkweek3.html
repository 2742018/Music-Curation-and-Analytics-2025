Task 2: Comparison of MusicXML and MEI Encoding

To better understand how my Great Fairy Fountain transcription is represented in two different encoding standards, I compared three core XML elements in the MusicXML file with their equivalents in the MEI file: the note element, the measure element, and the clef element. Examining these side by side highlights differences in naming conventions, attribute usage, structural organisation, and the intended purpose of each standard.

1. Notes (<note> in MusicXML vs <note> in MEI)

  • In MusicXML, the <note> element contains nested child elements such as <pitch> (with <step>, <alter>, <octave>), <duration>, <voice>, and <type>. These structures reflect MusicXML’s focus on engraving precision and accurate playback across notation software.
  • In MEI, the <note> element stores key information directly as attributes (e.g., @pname, @oct, @dur). MEI also supports additional editorial and analytical annotation, offering a more flexible and descriptive model.
  • Overall, MusicXML is more modular and explicit, while MEI is more compact and descriptive, reflecting its scholarly focus.

2. Measures (<measure> in both standards)

  • In MusicXML, <measure> elements include measure numbers and contain notation-related child elements such as <attributes>, <direction>, and <note>. This structure provides high interoperability between notation applications.
  • In MEI, <measure> can also carry identifiers but may appear within more complex hierarchical structures that support variants, editorial layers, or alternative readings. This design aligns with MEI’s emphasis on critical editing and detailed encoding of musical sources.
  • MEI therefore accommodates richer contextual information, whereas MusicXML focuses on a single, clear representation intended for performance and software compatibility.

3. Clefs (<clef> elements)

  • In MusicXML, clefs appear within the <attributes> element and include <sign> and <line> child elements to specify clef type and staff position.
  • In MEI, clefs are represented using attributes such as @shape and @line, and can be integrated more flexibly across staves, layers, and editorial contexts.
  • This distinction highlights MEI’s adaptable document structure compared with MusicXML’s straightforward representation for consistent rendering across notation tools.

In summary, both MusicXML and MEI successfully encode the musical content of my Koji Kondo transcription. But they still do so with different priorities: MusicXML is optimised for interoperability and playback, while MEI provides a more expressive, flexible model suited for scholarly, archival, and editorial work.
